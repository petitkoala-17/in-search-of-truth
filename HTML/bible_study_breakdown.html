1. Breakdown

Emphasizing the idea that everyone is either in darkness or in light creates a dichotomy that may lead individuals to feel judged based on their perceived sinfulness. However, the Bible itself contains nuances and gray areas that complicate such black-and-white thinking.

For example, in the listing of specific sins in Galatians 5:19-21, which in hindsight I understand why it repeatedly came up in our conversations, it is used to prove this point.


Galatians 5:19-21 lists drunkenness as a sin, leading some to claim that Christians should not drink alcohol at all. However, Jesus' first miracle in John 2:1-11 celebrates its use in joyful, appropriate settings.

Paul also advised Timothy to use wine for his stomach ailments in 1 Timothy 5:23, which states:

"Stop drinking only water, and use a little wine because of your stomach and your frequent illnesses."

This proves that the sin mentioned in Galatians refers to excess and loss of control, not the mere consumption of wine itself. The Bible promotes balance and discernment, not rigid extremes.


Another example: When Jesus heals a crippled woman on the Sabbath in Luke 13:10-17, it was seen as breaking Sabbath laws by the Pharisees, who had a black-and-white approach. Jesus highlighted that freeing this woman from suffering took priority over rigid legalism, showing a gray area.


Marriage and divorce: Jesus taught that divorce was permitted in the time of Moses due to the hardness of people's hearts, but it was not intended from the beginning. It is allowed in cases of infidelity (Matthew 19:8-9), and 1 Corinthians 7:10-16 provides another gray area: allowing divorce if an unbelieving spouse chooses to leave the marriage.


War and killing: In Joshua 6, God commands war, yet in the Ten Commandments, we are told not to kill. Similarly, in Luke 22:35-36, Jesus tells his disciples to carry swords, but later in Matthew 26:52 he advises against their use.


From these examples, we learn that the Bible does not operate in absolutes, but in balance. Ecclesiastes 3:1-8 states it best:

"There is a time for everything, and a season for every activity under the heavens: a time to be born and a time to die, a time to plant and a time to uproot... a time to tear and a time to mend, a time to be silent and a time to speak..."

Dealing in absolutes creates fear of judgment for failing to conform, pushing members to strictly adhere to agendas hidden behind doctrines.


The notion that everyone has sinned (which is true) diminishes individuality and fosters collective guilt, setting up the next part of recruitment.

The group teaches that sin separates one from God, and salvation can only be found within them, reinforcing urgency for redemption through the group.

Using examples like "worldly vs Godly sorrow" manipulates emotions, creating fear and obligation to change, often not from genuine belief but from fear of consequences.

The challenge to pick a baptism date at the end of this study instills urgency, pushing individuals to make quick decisions without considering their family or long-standing friends.

Reflecting on our experiences can lead to profound personal growth. It is important to recognize that every relationship has its challenges, but it is how we navigate those challenges that helps us grow stronger.

In any given relationship—family, friends, lovers, colleagues—there will always be quarrels and discord of some form and degree. There is no relationship without these things ever occurring. In the case of lovers, my research led me to find that these relationships always have 3 phases.

An initial phase after just meeting the person, where the spark is bright and everything is bliss. A second phase, where after some time together, flaws and shortcomings begin to appear, leading to conflict (anger/resentment), and this is where these kinds of relationships are either made or broken. For those that reach the third phase, there is a mutual understanding of who they are with and how to navigate the relationship, making it just as beautiful, if not more so, than it was at the start.   • The 3 Phases of Relationships by Greg Douglas, LPC, LMHC
