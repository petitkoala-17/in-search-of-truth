What is a cult?

A cult can be defined as: a group or movement that exerts an excessive level of devotion to a person, idea, or organization, often using manipulative and authoritarian practices. These groups aim to control members by isolating them from independent thought or external influences, thereby enforcing a belief system that claims superiority and/or exclusivity.

Contrary to popular belief, cults are not always cloaked, mysterious societies hiding and operating in secrecy. They are not always easily recognizable, often capable of existing in plain sight within our midst, operating under the guise of normality. They often present themselves as an innocent and benevolent community, masking their coercive tactics under the guise of being able to provide deeper spiritual or personal fulfillment which cannot be found elsewhere. Cults are often led by a single authoritarian figure, or small group of leaders, that demands unwavering loyalty and obedience from its members.

Examples of Cults

Over time, there have been plenty of cults to rise and fall, ranging from well-known and violent cults, to some much less obvious, and operating in a harmful yet less outwardly violent way.

Violent Cults

  1. The Manson Family: This cult was led by Charles Manson, and gained notoriety for the brutal murders carried out by their members in 1969. Manson effectively manipulated his followers into committing these heinous acts through the exploitation of psychological vulnerabilities, and promotions of doomsday prophecies.
  2. Heaven's Gate: Led by Marshall Applewhite, this cult combined science fiction and Christian theology. In 1997, 39 of their members committed mass suicide, holding the belief that they would ascend to a higher plane of existence.
    The members exhibited extreme devotion to their leader and his installed belief system, effectively cutting them off from ties with the outside world.
  3. Jonestown: This cult was led by Jim Jones. The group, ended in a mass-murder suicide in 1978 in Jonestown, Guyana.
    Over 900 people died after being coerced into drinking cyanide poisoned punch. Jones demanded absolute loyalty and isolated members from external influences, pushing them to deadly groupthink/hivemind mentality.

Subtle Cults

  1. The Children of God (Family International): This cult was initially founded in the 1960s by David Berg and claimed to offer spiritual salvation. However, it was later discovered that they engaged in coercive practices, manipulation, and sexual abuse.
    The members were isolated from outside ideas, and greatly encouraged to break ties with family members who were not part of the group. You can read more about this here.
  2. The Branch Davidians: This group was an off-shoot of the Seventh-Day Adventist Church, led by David Koresh. It operated as relatively "normal" religious group, with intense devotions, strict control and secrecy.
    However, the group's life ended in a 51-day standoff with law enforcement authorities in Waco, Texas in 1993.
  3. The Boston Movement (ICOC): This group has been criticized for using cult-like recruitment practices, such as authoritarian leadership, exclusivity, and isolation of members from external relationships.
    The members were pressured into confessing personal sins to their leaders and were taught to prioritize the church above all else, even family and career. Several former members have come forward over time, to shed light on the activities of the group and negative impacts it had on their lives.

Common Characteristics of Cults

Many cults share a common set of behaviors, tactics, and characteristics that set them apart from typical religious or social groups:

  • Authoritarian Leadership: One or a few leaders exert total control, claiming divine or exclusive knowledge. This is done with the aim of creating maximum dependency, and making members believe they are incapable of living an individual life outside the norms set by the group and its leaders.
  • Control Over Personal Lives: Members are encouraged to sever ties with outsiders and have their personal decisions micromanaged: from who they can interact with, where they can work, finances, and who they can pursue relationships with.
  • Manipulation through Fear: Threats of punishment, either physical or spiritual, are used to keep members compliant.
  • Isolation from "Outsiders": Members are isolated from friends, family, or anyone not part of the group to limit external influences either before they fully commit to joining. Sometimes this process starts immediately after joining in a gradual manner. This helps creating a hive-mind environment.
  • Absolute Loyalty: Any dissent is discouraged, and loyalty to the group is prioritized over all else.
  • Zero Tolerance for Questions: Questions and criticism are harshly met, and the individuals are accused of "losing faith", sometimes even punished for this.

Common Tactics Used by Cults

Cults often employ a set of tactics designed to recruit members, control their thoughts, and maintain their influence:

  • Assumption of Neediness: Here you are made to feel like "a broken bird" and "unwhole". They then make you believe only they have the capacity to make you whole again.
  • Definitive Answers: They claim to have the definitive answers and solutions to life's questions and problems. Questions and problems which you most likely happened to have been worried about.
  • Thought Reform: Cults use indoctrination or repetitive teachings to alter a member's worldview and beliefs to align them with the group's beliefs.
  • Behavior Control: Strict rules governing how members act, dress, and who they can/cannot interact with are put in place to maintain control.
  • Information Control: Members are encouraged to avoid consuming information that contradicts the group's beliefs, creating an isolated information bubble.
  • Emotional Manipulation: Fear, guilt, and shame are used to maintain obedience and loyalty.
  • Brainwashing: A systematic effort to persuade nonbelievers (recruits) to accept a certain allegiance, command, or doctrine.

Why do People Choose to Join A Cult?

This is an interesting question because We must first understand: no cult member openly approaches a potential recruit and overtly tells them they are part of cult and wish for them to join too. This is obviously counterproductive. Why then do people join?:

  • They feel "out of place" in the world.
  • They have a deep desire for meaning and community
  • Building on this "out of place" feeling and vulnerable state, they are in search for answers they feel they could never find.
  • The feeling of loneliness they harbor is presented with "a light at the end of the tunnel" invitation from the recruiter, to join their group which would provide all the answers they seek. This obviously makes them less suspicious and accept the strange elements of their new communities.
  • Most people simply don't realize they are joining one, and are discouraged from doing research on it.
  • The heavy manipulation involved in the recruitment process.
  • Unresolved insecurities they may have which have not been talked about or addressed. Again, "light at the end of the tunnel" scenario.
